Executive Summary

The v2 features-and-flows spec describes a working product on the happy path. This document interrogates everything else — what's missing, what breaks under stress, how the experience feels, and what design discipline keeps it coherent.

It is organised as six sections, each answering a specific question. A · Gaps — what features are missing? B · Corner cases — what weird situations break the spec? C · User journeys — what does the experience feel like over time? D · Service blueprints — who does what backstage to produce the experience? E · Design principles — what to remember when in doubt. F · P0 checklist — the single page that says what must ship before customer one.

Read in order if you have an hour. Read sections F → A → C if you have ten minutes. Companion to the v2 features & flows document and the Nepal market thesis memo.

Top 10 critical insights

If you read nothing else, read these. The most consequential conclusions from the audit, in priority order.

01
Offline mode is the real moat
Nepali clinics drop internet daily. A cloud SaaS that doesn't work offline gets rejected at month one — regardless of how good its features are. This is the single biggest P0 in the entire document.
02
First seven days post-go-live decide everything
SaaS in Nepal die during onboarding, not during build. Dedicated CS handholding, done-for-you Excel migration, and 7-day intensive support is non-negotiable. Spec must include this as a process, not a feature.
03
The recall loop is the retention engine
Chronic-disease recall (Flow 4) converts a one-time visit into a five-year relationship. It is the difference between $40 and $400 per-patient LTV. Build it for MVP, not Wave 3.
04
Voice-recorded ambient consult is the differentiator
Nothing in the Nepali market has it. Doctors hate typing in English on Devanagari keyboards. Ambient capture + AI visit notes is the highest-impact UX moment in the product — defensible against every local incumbent.
05
Tally export determines whether owners stay
Every clinic's accountant lives in Tally Prime. Without a clean export, the owner has to do double-entry — and will silently churn within six months. Boring; non-negotiable.
06
Triage is a P0, not a luxury
The current spec assumes first-come-first-served at the doctor. Real clinics need emergency / pediatric / elderly to jump the queue — with visible reasons so other patients don't lose trust.
07
External report ingestion is trust-building, not a feature
Patients use multiple clinics. Welcoming their external reports — instead of forcing them to start over — signals respect and removes the social friction of "telling on themselves." Clinical continuity follows.
08
Granular permissions matter more than you think
Discount limits, refund authorisation, sensitive-case access, owner-only financial views — without these, fraud and gossip become real risks in a small-team clinic environment. The owner's trust in the system depends on it.
09
Identity is messier than the spec assumes
Same patient registers under three names. Mobile is shared by a family. Children inherit a parent's number. Patient changes their name. Without fuzzy matching and family linking from day one, the patient base degrades into garbage in 18 months.
10
Family-level account is the referral engine
A satisfied chronic-care patient brings 2–4 family members. Family linking is the lowest-CAC growth channel available — and the product should make it one tap, not a settings-menu chore.

A·1 · Identity, registration & consent
The spec assumes "search by mobile, register if new." Reality is messier — duplicates, shared phones, minors, foreigners.
  • P0Duplicate-patient detection. Fuzzy match on name + DOB + mobile before allowing new registration."Ram Bahadur Shrestha" on three different mobile numbers from three different visits — all the same person.Soundex + Levenshtein on name + DOB match → soft-warning duplicate flag at registration.
  • P1Shared-mobile handling. Multiple family members on one phone is the Nepali norm.Husband, wife, two children all share one mobile. Patient App OTP login resolves to "which family member?""This number is shared by N people" flag with disambiguation at sign-in; family-linked profiles.
  • P1Minor / guardian consent. Patient under 18 needs guardian relationship and consent.
  • P1Patient identity verification at sample collection.Two patients with similar names. Tech draws blood from the wrong one. Single most-common diagnostic error.Two-factor verification at draw — name + DOB read aloud, barcode scanned against the active order.
  • P2National ID / Nagarik App integration as optional secondary identifier.
  • P2Foreign nationals with passport-based ID — no PAN, often no Nepali mobile.
  • P2Privacy preferences per patient. Opt-out of marketing, WhatsApp, analytics.
  • P1Right-to-be-forgotten flow per Privacy Act 2018.
  • P2Patient access request — full record export. SLA, auth, format spec.
A·2 · Clinical workflow depth
The spec stops at "doctor sees patient, prescribes." Real clinical work has triage, nurses, weight-based dosing, special populations, multi-visit care plans.
  • P0Triage / priority queue. Emergency, pediatric, elderly should jump the queue.A 78-year-old with chest pain is told to wait behind 12 routine consults because tokens are FCFS.Priority levels with visible reasons displayed to other waiting patients ("Patient ahead was an emergency").
  • P0Nurse / vitals-tech step before doctor. Most clinics take vitals separately first. The flow conflates this with the doctor.
  • P1Pre-consultation symptom intake. Patient fills on phone before walking in; doctor sees pre-populated form.
  • P1Pediatric weight-based dosing. Drug calculator must take weight as input.3-year-old, 14 kg, paracetamol — "10–15 mg/kg q6h." Doctor needs the calculator inline, not in their head.
  • P1Pregnancy contraindication alerts. Pregnant patient + teratogenic drug = hard alert.
  • P2Renal / hepatic dose adjustment based on lab values.
  • P2Care plan across visits. Currently each visit is discrete; chronic care needs a longitudinal plan with goals.
  • P2Disease registries. Diabetic, hypertension, with population-level dashboards.
  • P2Procedure consent forms templated, signed, stored.
  • P2Discharge summary template for clinics doing day-care.
  • P3Group consultations (family planning, antenatal classes).
A·3 · Prescription & medication management
Writing a prescription is the easy part. Refills, controlled substances, hand-off to pharmacy, drug recalls — none are in the spec.
  • P0Prescription-to-pharmacy hand-off.Doctor prescribes; what happens next? In-house pharmacy auto-receives? Patient takes paper to external pharmacy? Currently undefined.Configurable per clinic: in-house auto-route, external-print-only, or patient-choice.
  • P1Refill workflow. Doctor sets "refill × 3 months" at original prescribing.
  • P1Controlled-substances ledger per DDA. Legal liability if missing.
  • P1Generic substitution consent. Pharmacy may substitute; doctor explicitly allows or disallows.
  • P2Patient drug-instruction sheet auto-printed in Nepali.
  • P2Drug recall handling. Identify all patients on recalled batch.
  • P3Compound preparations for specialised pharmacies.
A·4 · Diagnostic depth
Pathology is multi-day. Microbiology is multi-day. The spec assumes same-visit results.
  • P1Microbiology workflow. Culture → preliminary → sensitivity → final. 3–5 day cycle.
  • P1Histopathology / cytology workflow. Grossing → processing → embedding → staining → reporting. Multi-step.
  • P1Reflex testing. "If TSH abnormal, auto-add Free T3/T4."
  • P1Delta check. Flag implausibly large change from prior result.
  • P2Test panels with discount. Lipid profile, LFT priced as panel.
  • P2Result amendment after release with patient notification.
  • P2Add-on test from existing sample within stability window.
  • P3Reference range customisation for paediatric vs adult vs geriatric.
A·5 · Billing & financial sophistication
"Bill cart, pay, receipt" works for cash. Real clinics deal with insurance, corporates, packages, refunds, accounting integration.
  • P0VAT-exempt vs taxable split on receipt. Medical consultation largely VAT-exempt; pharmacy is taxable. IRD audits this.
  • P1NHIP claim packet generation for HIB-enrolled patients.
  • P1SSF claim flow for formal-sector employees.
  • P1Private insurance pre-authorisation.
  • P1Corporate billing for monthly aggregated invoices.
  • P1Package billing. Maternity, surgery — fixed price across visits.
  • P1Refund / partial refund flow with full audit trail.
  • P2Bad debt / write-off with formal authorisation.
  • P2Nepali fiscal year close (mid-July to mid-July, BS-aware).
  • P2Multi-cashier with separate drawers.
  • P3Foreign currency invoicing for foreign patients.
A·6 · Pharmacy module
Mentioned in Wave 3 of Front Desk but never specified. In-house pharmacy is a major revenue line for many Nepali clinics.
  • P1Drug master with Nepal SKUs (NDA-aligned brand-generic mapping).
  • P1Stock with batch and expiry; FEFO dispensing.
  • P1Dispensing log per pharmacist.
  • P1Purchase orders, vendor master, GRN, invoice match.
  • P1Auto-reorder thresholds.
  • P1Expiry alerts (60/30/15/7-day).
  • P2Cold-chain monitoring for vaccines, insulin, biologics.
  • P2OTC walk-in sales separate from prescription dispensing.
  • P2Stock transfer between branches.
  • P2Damage / wastage tracking.
  • P3DDA-format controlled-substance ledger for inspection.
A·7 · Roles, permissions & security
Front-desk / doctor / lab / owner is too coarse. Discount limits, refund auth, locum, sensitive cases, all need finer control.
  • P0Granular permissions. Discount limit per role; refund auth threshold; deletion rights; owner-only financials.
  • P02FA for staff login, especially owner/admin.
  • P1Multi-clinic users. Doctor at three clinics — context switch without re-login.
  • P1Locum / temp staff with time-limited access.
  • P1Sensitive-case access controls. HIV, mental health, abortion — restricted view.
  • P1Session timeout policies with auto-logout.
  • P1Failed-login lockout + alert.
  • P2Audit log retention & review interface.
  • P2Encryption at rest with documented key management.
  • P2Disaster recovery and backup retention policy with stated RTO/RPO.
  • P3Penetration test schedule with finding-remediation SLAs.
A·8 · Data governance & privacy
Privacy Act 2018, Telemedicine Guidelines 2077, EMR Directive 2081 — the spec is silent on all three.
  • P0Patient consent capture and versioning at registration, telemedicine, and on policy change.
  • P1Data localisation compliance. EMR Directive may mandate Nepal-resident hosting.
  • P1Data breach notification process — defined before there's a breach.
  • P2Granular consent for analytics, research, aggregated sharing.
  • P2De-identified data export for public-health partnerships.
  • P3Patient data portability via FHIR.

A·11 · Infrastructure, devices & offline mode
Nepali clinics lose internet and power regularly. The spec assumes always-on cloud — this is the single biggest assumption error.

B

Corner cases & edge conditions

Gaps are missing features. Corner cases are the weird situations that the existing features must survive without breaking. Most production failures live here. Eight categories, organised by who or what triggers the edge.

B·1 · Patient identity edges
Patients aren't tidy. They share phones, change names, lose phones, want anonymity, are minors, are foreign, are dying, or are dead.
  • P0Patient with no mobile phone.Elderly patient, daughter brings her in. Daughter's mobile is logged. Next year daughter moves abroad — mother loses access to her own records.Allow primary contact distinct from patient. On change, prompt update.
  • P0Patient changes name (marriage / legal change).Name history with effective dates; receipts/prescriptions show name as-of-visit.
  • P1Same name, different patients."Ram Bahadur Shrestha" — 47 patients in the system.Disambiguate by DOB + last 4 of mobile + photo if captured.
  • P1Anonymous testing requirement (HIV, STI, drug abuse).Pseudonym field, separate from primary record; opt-in linkage later.
  • P1Mass-casualty event registration.Earthquake or bus accident — 30 patients arrive in 20 minutes, half unconscious.Bulk emergency-mode registration with placeholder IDs (e.g. EMG-2026-04-29-001) reconciled later.
  • P1VIP / celebrity patient privacy.Pseudonym record; restricted access list; audit log specially flagged.
  • P2Refugee / undocumented patient with no national ID.
  • P2Patient impersonation / fraud detection.Brother uses sister's NHIP card to access free care.Photo + signature on file; mismatch triggers manual verification at front desk.
  • P1Deceased patient.Mark record deceased; block future appointment booking; preserve history; transfer family-link visibility per consent.
  • P2Minor turning 18. Parent loses default visibility; requires explicit re-grant.
B·2 · Clinical workflow edges
Real medicine is full of "actually, no, wait" moments — refusals, mid-treatment changes, conflicts, polypharmacy, language barriers, mental-health emergencies.
  • P1Doctor changes mind mid-prescription.Draft state until "save & print"; full edit allowed pre-finalisation; post-finalisation requires amendment.
  • P1Patient refuses treatment / test (informed refusal).Templated refusal note with patient signature; remains in record permanently.
  • P1Polypharmacy alerts.68-year-old on 7 chronic meds — adding an 8th risks interaction cascade.Soft alert at 5+ active meds; hard alert on confirmed interaction.
  • P1Drug shortage mid-treatment.Pharmacy flags out-of-stock; doctor notified; alternative suggested via drug-master.
  • P1Patient leaves AMA (against medical advice).AMA form with explicit acknowledgement; flag in record.
  • P1Language barrier — patient doesn't speak Nepali or English.Interpreter field, language preference flag, multilingual prescription print.
  • P0Mental-health emergency / suicide-risk disclosure.Crisis-protocol alert in Doc App; auto-display referral options; sensitive-case access controls automatically activated.
  • P1Domestic abuse disclosure.Confidential note; restricted-access flag; safe-discharge planning fields.
  • P1Notifiable disease (TB, dengue, COVID-19).Auto-route to EDCD reporting queue; surveillance flag in record.
  • P2Patient returns same day with new symptom.Same-day re-consultation flag; bills as separate or as continuation per clinic policy.
  • P2Two doctors with conflicting opinions.Both notes preserved; primary-treating-doctor designation; visible conflict flag.
  • P2Disability accommodations in scheduling and waiting (extra time, wheelchair, sign language).
  • P2Adverse drug event.ADE form with auto-link to prescribed drug; pharmacovigilance reporting trigger.
B·3 · Billing & payment edges
Cash-clean transactions are the easy 60%. The other 40% is partial pay, splits, mistakes, fraud, free-care eligibility, and accounting reconciliation.
  • P0Patient can't pay full amount.Bill is NPR 4,200; patient has NPR 1,500. Common.Partial-payment flow with outstanding ledger; due-date capture; SMS reminder for balance.
  • P0Wrong patient charged.Reversal flow with reason; original transaction preserved; new transaction audited.
  • P1Bill split between insurer and patient.Co-pay calculation; two-line settlement; insurer balance tracked separately.
  • P1Bipanna citizen / free-care eligibility.Free-care flag on patient profile; bill auto-zeros for eligible services; subsidy ledger maintained.
  • P1Senior citizen / disability discount.Auto-applied per government rules; visible on receipt with statutory reference.
  • P1Emergency-care-now-bill-later.Service first, deferred billing flag; balance becomes due on discharge or reconciliation.
  • P1Patient paid twice (system error or human).Duplicate-payment detection; proactive refund to original payment method.
  • P1Cash discrepancy at day-end.Counted cash NPR 12,400; system says NPR 12,800.Variance log; require reason and supervisor sign-off; pattern reporting to owner.
  • P1Refund to wallet vs cash vs bank.Refund-method match to original payment method by default; deviation requires supervisor approval.
  • P2Currency mix in single transaction (cash NPR + INR + card).
  • P2Payment-gateway settlement mismatch.Daily reconciliation report; settlement variance escalation.
  • P2Suspicious payment patterns.Same staff issuing 8 discounts above threshold in one day.Anomaly detection on discount/refund volumes; owner alert.
B·4 · Diagnostic & lab edges
Labs are mechanical and physical. Things contaminate, get lost, break, or expire. The system has to handle each gracefully.
  • P0Sample mix-up — wrong patient's blood drawn.Two patients with similar names; tech misreads label.Two-factor verification at draw; barcode scan against active order; mismatch alarm.
  • P0Sample contamination / haemolysis / clotting.Rejection workflow; auto-notify front desk for re-collection; no charge for repeat draw.
  • P1Sample lost in transit (send-out).Status tracking with stale-status alerts; partner-lab escalation; patient notification with new ETA.
  • P1Test result clearly wrong (delta-check trips).Hold release; alert tech for repeat; doctor sees "pending verification" state.
  • P0Critical value but doctor unreachable.Potassium 6.8, life-threatening; ordering doctor on flight to Pokhara.Escalation chain — primary doctor → on-call doctor → senior physician → clinic owner. SMS, call, app push in parallel; acknowledgement required.
  • P1Equipment malfunction mid-batch.Quarantine all results from affected period; QC re-run requirement; patient notification of delay.
  • P1Reagent expired during run.Auto-quarantine results; re-run with fresh reagent; QC documentation for inspection.
  • P1Power outage mid-test.Auto-save partial state; resume on power; inform tech which tests need re-run.
  • P1Result available after patient has left.Asynchronous delivery via app + SMS + WhatsApp; patient need not return.
  • P2Doctor wants add-on test on existing sample.Within-stability-window check per test; auto-enable add-on with billing reconciliation.
  • P2Pathologist on leave during a critical case.On-call substitution roster; verification workflow re-routes automatically.
  • P2Result amended after release.Original released report preserved; addendum issued; patient + doctor notified; original PDF marked superseded.
B·5 · Privacy, legal & access edges
Records are subpoenaed, requested, demanded, snooped on, and lost. The system needs a legally defensible answer for each.
  • P0Court / police subpoena for records.Documented release-of-records workflow with legal authorisation capture; full audit trail; patient notified unless court-ordered confidentiality.
  • P1Family member tries to access without authorisation.Estranged spouse calls front desk asking for last visit details.Default deny; consent-on-file required; verbal verification not sufficient.
  • P1Insurance company audit.De-identified bulk export by case number; consent verified; access logged.
  • P0Doctor leaves clinic — what happens to their patient relationships?Patient ownership transfers to clinic; doctor retains professional CME record; patients notified of doctor change.
  • P0Clinic shuts down.Data preservation for legally-required retention period; patient export option; orderly handover to successor clinic if any.
  • P0Cyberattack / ransomware.Documented IR plan; offline backups; breach notification per Privacy Act; restoration RTO defined.
  • P1Data exfiltration by employee.Bulk-export rate limits; export audit logs; review queue for unusual patterns.
  • P1Patient asks "what data do you have on me?"Self-service report in Patient App; full record export within 7 working days per Privacy Act.
  • P1Patient asks for deletion.Deletion workflow with retention exceptions (legal, fiscal); confirmation; soft-delete with hard-delete after retention period.
  • P2Minor reaches majority.Consent re-collection from young adult; parent visibility revoked unless re-granted.
  • P2Cross-border data request from foreign government for a foreign patient.
B·6 · Operational & infrastructure edges
Power, internet, equipment, staff, weather, politics — the operating environment in Nepal is unstable in ways the spec must absorb.
  • P0Internet outage (extended).Offline mode (see A·11); local sync queue; reconcile on reconnect.
  • P0Power outage.UPS recommendation in deployment guide; auto-save every action; recovery flow on restart.
  • P0Server downtime / SaaS provider outage.Status page; degraded-mode operation; SLA credit; communication template.
  • P1Backup failure (silent).Daily backup-verification job; immediate alert on failure; weekly restore-test.
  • P1Software bug discovered in production.Hotfix process; canary deployment; communication to affected customers within 24h.
  • P1Doctor sudden unavailability.Mass-reschedule with patient notification; redirect to colleague where appropriate.
  • P1Staff leaves with knowledge.Documented runbooks; knowledge-base contributions tracked; named-staff access decommissioned within 24h.
  • P1License expirations — clinic license, doctor's NMC, drug license.Expiry tracker per credential; warning at 90/60/30 days.
  • P2Earthquake / disaster.Disaster-recovery playbook; remote-hot-standby; mobile fallback; emergency-mode triage registration.
  • P2Pandemic / surge.Bulk-flag infection-control protocol; surge-mode triage; isolation-room scheduling support.
  • P2Festival / holiday closures.Calendar-aware scheduling; pre-festival appointment cap; recall-pause windows.
  • P2Political bandh / curfew.Bulk reschedule by date range; SMS notification with rebooking link.
B·7 · Customer lifecycle edges
SaaS customers don't all glide from sign-up to advocacy. Some go silent, some default, some sue, some want what's impossible.
  • P0Customer goes silent for 30 days.Health-score drop alert; CS outreach within 48h; usage diagnostic shared with customer.
  • P1Customer threatens to leave.Escalation to senior CS; root-cause interview; written remediation plan; renewal-discount discretion.
  • P1Customer churns.Exit-interview; data export within 14 days; full deletion within 90 days; lessons-learned to product roadmap.
  • P1Customer defaults on payment.Grace period (15 days); read-only mode (next 15 days); export-only mode (next 60 days); deletion thereafter.
  • P1Customer's clinic shuts down.Documented closure flow; data handover to successor or patient export; legal retention preserved.
  • P1Customer outgrows the product (chain expansion).Migration path to enterprise tier; feature gap roadmap; transparent timeline.
  • P2Customer wants white-label / reseller.Legal agreement template; revenue split; brand isolation in product.
  • P2Customer requests feature that conflicts with another customer's request.Public roadmap; transparent prioritisation criteria; configurability where the cost is justified.
  • P2Customer is referred fraud.Suspension flow; investigation; refund/clawback if needed.
  • P2Customer sues.Legal escalation; communication freeze; preservation hold on data.
B·8 · System integrity, concurrency & format edges
Real software collides with itself. Two users edit the same record. Devanagari encodes weird. Dates flip across BS-AD. Time zones are awkward.
  • P0Two users edit same record simultaneously.Doctor updates prescription; nurse updates vitals at the same second; one overwrites the other.Optimistic locking with version numbers; conflict UI for human resolution; field-level merge where possible.
  • P1Time zone correctness. Nepal is UTC+5:45 — odd offset. India sometimes confused.All timestamps stored UTC; display localised; explicit zone tag on exports.
  • P1BS / AD calendar sync issues.Patient born "2042 BS" — needs accurate AD conversion across leap years.Authoritative BS-AD conversion library; both displayed everywhere a date matters.
  • P1Devanagari encoding issues. Mixed scripts in patient names, copy-paste from Word.UTF-8 throughout; normalisation NFC; render test for top-200 Nepali names.
  • P1Long names truncated on receipts.Adaptive font-size; second-line wrap; full-name preserved in record.
  • P1Number formatting (lakhs vs millions; comma placement).Locale-aware formatting; user preference setting.
  • P1DOB unknown — only year known.Common for elderly Nepali patients.Accept partial dates; flag in record; age-calculation tolerates uncertainty.
  • P2Database migration / version upgrades.Zero-downtime migration patterns; backwards-compatible schema changes; rollback plan.
  • P2Clock drift on local devices.Server time as authoritative; client warns on significant drift.
  • P2Decimal precision in lab values.Per-test precision config; display rules; rounding policy documented.

A service blueprint maps what's visible to the customer, what's frontstage, what's backstage, and what support systems sit underneath. Three blueprints follow — one for each journey above. Each is the operational complement to its journey.

How to read a blueprint
Stages run left to right (time). Layers run top to bottom — customer at the top, deeper system layers below. The three black bars are the line of interaction (above, customer engaged), line of visibility (above, customer-visible), and line of internal interaction (above, staff; below, infrastructure). Where the journey says "she felt anxious," the blueprint shows the eight backstage processes that resolved the cause.

Why three blueprints, not just one
The acute episode (Blueprint 1) is the product's most-frequent service. The chronic-care loop (Blueprint 2) is the product's most-valuable service — it's where LTV expands. The onboarding flow (Blueprint 3) is the product's most-fragile service — it's where customers die. Each is a different kind of operational complexity, and each has to be designed deliberately.
E

Design principles

When you can't decide between two ways of building something, return to these. They're the synthesis of every gap, corner case, and journey above.

1
Offline is a first-class state, not a fallback.
If a feature only works online, it doesn't work in Nepal. Every Front Desk and Doc App action must work offline and reconcile cleanly. The cloud is for sync, not for prerequisite.
2
Trust beats features.
A clinic owner switching to a new system is making a trust-bet. Audit logs, granular permissions, transparent error messages, and visible CS responsiveness build trust. Five extra features beat by one extra unexplained outage.
3
Clinical safety before clinical convenience.
Allergy alerts. Drug interactions. Critical-value escalation. Sample-mix-up prevention. Triage. Build these before you build a beautifully-styled prescription printer. The order you build features in encodes your priorities.
4
Localise hard, not soft.
Nepali UI is necessary; not sufficient. BS calendar, NPR currency, NMC numbers, FonePay, Tally export, IRD invoice format, MoHP HMIS reporting — these are the hard localisation. Skip them and you've built an Indian product.
5
Every patient interaction must feel remembered.
Trend graphs that include external data. Pre-filled symptom forms. Recall messages timed to the patient's actual condition. The one thing a paper system cannot do — and the one thing the product must do consistently.
6
Backstage exists to make frontstage graceful.
A patient should never see "queue calculation failed." Every backstage failure has a frontstage degraded-mode behaviour. Service-blueprint failures must have rehearsed responses, not spontaneous panic.
7
First seven days post-go-live is the product.
The features matter; the onboarding matters more. Done-for-you Excel migration, Nepali training videos, real-time WhatsApp support — these are not "customer success extras," they are part of the product itself.
8
Let customers leave cleanly.
A churning customer is a referral risk. Full data export, dignified deletion, no last-minute hassles. The grace with which you let people leave determines what they tell others about you.
9
Design for shared phones, shared mobiles, and family-as-unit.
The Western "one user, one device, one account" assumption fails immediately. Family-linked profiles, multi-patient OTP disambiguation, and primary-contact distinct from patient must be P0 — not patched in later.
10
Boring infrastructure is the moat.
Tally export. Thermal-printer support. UPS-friendly auto-save. eSewa reconciliation. None of these are pitch-deck features. They are the difference between a product that ships and a product that survives.
